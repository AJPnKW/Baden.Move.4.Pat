# Decision Log

| Date Window | Decision Area | Decision Summary | Why It Mattered |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Early planning | Move scope | Treat the effort as both a move and a transition project | Renovation, staging, and logistics were interdependent |
| Pre-possession | Renovation timing | Separate must-do-before-move work from deferable work | Protected readiness for possession and initial occupancy |
| Overlap period | Transport method | Split mover work from self-move work | Reduced schedule pressure and improved control |
| Pre-cutover | Utility and service timing | Sequence service transfers around occupancy readiness | Reduced downtime and move-day friction |
| Throughout | Item disposition | Use sell, donate, discard, store, and move categories | Reduced clutter and supported staged transition |
| Throughout | Planning model | Use a compact working artifact set instead of heavyweight formal PM packages | Preserved control without creating administrative overload |
| Research phase | Option validation | Use trial work to validate options and refine scope where needed | Allowed requirements to emerge from real constraints rather than assumptions |
| Renovation analysis | Bathroom / laundry decisions | Evaluate design, fixture, furnishing, and readiness implications together | Prevented isolated choices from breaking the larger workflow |
| Cutover window | Day-specific execution framing | Use day -1, day 0, day +1, and day +2 checkpoints | Helped make timing-specific work concrete and manageable |

## Decision Logic

The project did not assume every decision could be optimized perfectly. Many decisions were made under bounded time, limited certainty, and real downstream dependency pressure.

The working standard was:

- make the next decision at the right level of detail
- preserve future options where possible
- lock down decisions when a delay would create greater risk than imperfection
